# 1. Introduction
This project investigates how biological concepts can provide an alternative framework for understanding contemporary computational systems by adopting biological morphology as a visual index and translating biological principles into computational behaviours and visual expressions.

Rather than approaching computation solely as a technical process, the project explores biological systems as conceptual models for interaction, organisation and emergence. By adopting biological morphology as a visual index and translating biological principles into computational behaviours, the project examines how biological thinking can reshape the relationship between humans and digital systems. Based on biological structures and behaviours, they are abstracted into visual language, interaction design, and algorithmic rules.

# 2. Research
## Biological Research
The project adopts the cell as its primary biological reference. A cell is the fundamental structural and functional unit of life. Rather than selecting the cell simply for its visual appearance, the project is interested in the organisational principles that cells embody: individuality, communication, adaptation and continuous transformation. The appearance of cell membranes, nuclei, transparency and organic movement informs the visual design of the digital organisms.

## Cell Communication
Cells constantly exchange information through signalling processes that enable coordination, adaptation and collective behaviour. This biological principle is translated into the installation through participant interaction.

Each participant contributes two different forms of biological information:
- Facial data generates the visual identity of a synthetic cell.
- Voice data influences its behavioural characteristics.

Rather than communicating through biochemical signals, synthetic cells communicate through computational rules, producing an evolving network of relationships inside the virtual Petri dish.

## Symbiosis
Symbiosis describes relationships in which different organisms coexist while continuously influencing one another. The installation adopts this principle as its central interaction model.

Each participant contributes an individual digital organism to a shared ecosystem. Existing cells continue to drift, gather, separate and merge over time. No single participant controls the outcome; instead, the ecosystem emerges through the accumulated participation of many individuals.  Every participant contributes a fragment of themselves to a computational ecology that continues evolving beyond their individual interaction.

## Evolution
Unlike a fixed artwork, the installation continuously changes through participation. Every newly generated cell introduces new behavioural variables into the ecosystem, altering the relationships between existing organisms. The resulting ecology remains open-ended, producing visual and behavioural configurations that cannot be fully predicted. The project therefore explores emergence rather than control.
